Geography

Location

The Tlingit tribe lives in southeastern Alaska, mainly in the Alaska Panhandle. Their land includes many islands and a small part of the mainland.


Important Tlingit cities are Juneau, Sitka, and Ketchikan. The Tlingit territory stretches along the coast and nearby islands in the Pacific Ocean.

Environment

Climate

The Tlingit region has a temperate rainforest climate. It gets a lot of rain, with about 54 inches falling each year. Winters are cool, with temperatures around 30°F, while summers are mild, reaching about 60°F.


The weather is often misty and damp, but the temperatures don't change much between seasons. This climate supports lush forests and diverse marine life.

Culture

Customs & Traditions

Tlingit people are divided into two main groups called moieties: Raven and Eagle. Each moiety has different clans that trace their family lines through mothers — a matrilineal society. They hold special events called potlatches, where they give gifts to guests. These gatherings are important for showing social status and building community ties.


Tlingit people also have a rich tradition of storytelling and art, including totem pole carving and basket weaving.

Dress

Traditional Clothing

Tlingit people traditionally wore clothes made from animal skins and furs. They created special robes called Chilkat Robes, woven from mountain goat wool and cedar bark.


They also wore copper shields called Tináa as necklaces to show wealth and status. Their clothing often had beautiful designs that were meaningful to their culture, including clan symbols and nature motifs.

Key Garments
  • Chilkat Robes — Woven from mountain goat wool & cedar bark
  • Tináa — Copper shields worn as status symbols
  • Animal Furs — Everyday wear for warmth and protection

Cuisine

Traditional Food

Tlingit traditional food comes from the people who live in Alaska and British Columbia. They mainly eat seafood, especially salmon, which they catch using special fishing methods. The Tlingit also gather shellfish and sea plants from the ocean.


When they hunt animals, they use every part — including bones for soup and organs for nutrition. They also collect seasonal berries and plants, making their meals healthy and full of local tastes.

Architecture

Shelter & Housing

Tlingit housing is made from wood, especially from large cedar trees. They live in big homes called longhouses, which can fit several families and have a fire in the middle for cooking and warmth.


Inside, they use things like moss and bark to keep the house warm. The longhouse design shows how families are connected and live together — a reflection of how the Tlingit people are close to nature and to each other.

Longhouse Facts

Built from cedar planks • Houses multiple families • Central fire for warmth & cooking • Decorated with intricate clan carvings • Some up to 60 feet long

Technology

Tools & Natural Resources

The Tlingit people used natural resources and tools to help them survive. They made tools from wood, stone, and copper for fishing, hunting, and making things. Cedar trees were important for building their homes and making canoes, while stones were shaped into knives and other tools.


The Tlingit gathered food like salmon, berries, and shellfish from the land and sea. Their understanding of nature helped them create useful tools and find what they needed to live.

Commerce

Trade Items

The Tlingit people traded many items to get things they needed but could not find in their area. They exchanged goods like dried fish, furs from animals, and cedar bark for items such as caribou skins and copper.


Their location near the ocean allowed them to gather seafood, which was a big part of their trade. The Tlingit also traded during certain seasons when they had extra goods to share. This trading helped them connect with other tribes and get important resources for their daily lives.

Oral Tradition

Myths & Legends

Tlingit myths tell stories about animals and spirits that explain natural events and cultural practices.

How Raven Brought Light to the World

Long ago, the world was dark all the time. Raven, who was both a bird and a powerful spirit, heard that a rich man had light hidden in his house. Raven decided to steal this light to bring it to the world.

Raven turned himself into a tiny seed and fell into the water that the rich man's daughter was drinking. She swallowed the seed, and Raven was born as her grandfather's grandson. As a baby, Raven cried until his grandfather gave him the boxes that held the light.

Raven opened these boxes one by one, releasing stars, the moon, and finally the sun into the sky. With light now in the world, Raven flew away, bringing day and night to everyone.

Did You Know?

Fun Facts

01

The Tlingit people have lived in Alaska for over 10,000 years, making them one of the oldest indigenous groups in the region.

02

Tlingit society is divided into two main groups — Raven (or Crow) and Eagle (or Wolf). Traditionally, people from one moiety had to marry from the other.

03

The Tlingit are known for their totem poles. Being "low on the totem pole" doesn't mean low status — important figures are often at the bottom!

04

Tlingit people lived in large wooden plank houses called clan houses, which could hold several families and were decorated with intricate carvings.

05

They are skilled woodworkers — the killer whale carving symbolizes strength and protection in Tlingit culture.

06

The Tlingit have a matrilineal society, meaning family lines and inheritance are traced through the mother's side.

07

They were master traders, traveling far along the coast to exchange furs, cedar bark, and seafood with other tribes.

08

Traditional Tlingit cuisine relies heavily on salmon, which they preserve through smoking and drying techniques.

09

The potlatch is an important ceremonial gathering involving feasts, dances, and gift-giving to display wealth and social status.

10

Tlingit people used massive canoes made from red cedar — some up to 60 feet long — for fishing and trading expeditions along the coast.
